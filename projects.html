Written Communication

Professional Email Activity

Item 3 - My Reply to Ms. Marilla

To: Marcia Marilla, Sales Office Manager

From: Brayden Benard, Sales Intern

Date: March 31, 2026

Subject: Re: Revise, ASAP! - Feedback and Revised Draft Attached

Dear Ms. Marilla,

Thank you for trusting me with this task. I have reviewed the draft carefully and prepared the three items you requested, all included in this document.

Item 1 - Annotated Draft: The original email follows on the next page with suggested deletions shown in red strikethrough, suggested insertions in bold blue, and editorial comments in red italics explaining the reasoning behind each change.

Item 2 - Fully Rewritten Email: After the annotated draft you will find a completely rewritten version of the dress-code message, maintaining your core goals while adopting a more professional tone and structure.

My overall assessment is that the draft's message is important and your instinct to involve the team in creating a solution is excellent. The main areas for revision are: (1) the subject line, which is alarming rather than informative; (2) a tone that sometimes reads as mocking rather than corrective; (3) excessive capitalization and informal punctuation that undercut authority; and (4) a closing that contradicts the seriousness of the message. The rewritten version addresses all of these while keeping your collaborative spirit intact.

Please let me know if you'd like any adjustments before the 4 p.m. deadline.

Best regards,
Brayden Benard

Item 2 - Fully Rewritten Email

To: Sales Team - Carrollton and McKinney Offices

From: Marcia Marilla, Sales Office Manager

Date: March 28, 2026

Subject: Follow-Up on CEO Visit - Dress Code Input Requested by March 29

Dear Team,

As many of you are aware, our CEO visited both the Carrollton and McKinney offices last week. The visit was a positive one overall, and I want to thank everyone for your continued strong performance. However, she shared a concern with me regarding professional appearance that I want to address directly and transparently.

During her visit, she observed attire that did not meet the standard expected of a client-facing sales environment. Items noted included hoodies, graphic T-shirts, gym shorts, crop tops, and open-toed sandals. This feedback was shared with me personally, and I take it seriously. How we present ourselves reflects the professionalism and confidence that defines our team's success.

Rather than issuing a dress code policy unilaterally, I would like to hear from you. I believe we can build something fair and practical together, and your perspective will help ensure the final guidelines are clear and reasonable for everyone.

Please consider the following questions as you formulate your response:

  1. What attire do you consider appropriate for a professional sales office environment?
  2. Are there specific items - such as leggings, sandals, or denim - you feel should be addressed explicitly in a written policy?
  3. Should the dress standard differ when clients are scheduled to visit the office?
  4. Is there a role for a Casual Friday policy, and if so, how should its boundaries be defined?
  5. Would a written reference guide or checklist be helpful, particularly for new team members?

Please reply to this email with your thoughts by 10 a.m. on Saturday, March 29. I will compile all input and share a draft policy early next week for review before it is formally implemented.

I appreciate your professionalism and your commitment to representing our offices well. This team's results speak for themselves - and the way we show up every day, in every sense, is part of what makes that possible.

Best regards,
Marcia Marilla
Sales Office Manager
Carrollton and McKinney Sales Offices

What this was: My supervisor asked me - as a new intern - to proofread an email she was about to send to the whole sales team about a dress code complaint from the CEO. The catch was that her draft was not great. She had written it at the end of the week, and it showed. My job was to give her annotated feedback with tracked changes, write a professional reply, and produce a fully rewritten version for her to consider sending.

What I figured out: The grammar issues were easy to fix. The harder part was diagnosing why the email felt off even when individual sentences were technically fine. The core problem was tone - she was frustrated, and it came through in ways that would have made people defensive instead of receptive. Once I identified that the real issue was emotional register rather than just word choice, the rewrite got a lot easier. I stopped trying to polish the original and started over with a different goal: make people want to help solve the problem instead of avoid it.

Why it stuck with me: I'm going to be asked to write things on behalf of other people throughout my career - documentation, summaries, emails to clients. Knowing how to represent someone else's intent while still making the communication actually work is a skill I'll use constantly, and this was a good first real practice rep.

02

Oral / Presentation

The Unspoken Rules - Team Presentation

About This Presentation

Title

The Unspoken Rules

Team

Brayden Benard, Aaron Luong, Zach Darrow, Isaac Weston

Covered: Internal vs. External Narrative · Past-Present-Future storytelling · The 3 C's (Commitment, Compatibility, Competence) · Professional Appearance

What this was: My group presented to the class on how to tell your professional story - specifically the gap between how you think about your own career and how you need to frame it for an interview or a new job. We had 8 slides and about 15 minutes. My piece was the Past-Present-Future framework, which is basically the structure behind every good "tell me about yourself" answer.

What actually happened: The content wasn't the hard part - we all understood the material. The hard part was making it feel like one presentation instead of four separate mini-talks stitched together. We did a full run-through the night before and cut two slides we didn't have time to actually explain. That was the right call, even if it was annoying in the moment. The Q&A went better than expected because we had talked through the material enough times that nobody froze up.

What I took from it: Good team presentations require everyone to actually know the whole deck, not just their own section. I kept having to explain context from earlier slides during the Q&A, and it reminded me that an audience doesn't know who's responsible for what - they just see the team. That accountability stuck with me.

Sleep & Screen Time - 6 Week Tracking Study

The Numbers

4

6

3

Charts built

Sleep · Screen time · Daily steps · Mood (1-5) · Jan 13 - Feb 23, 2025

What I tracked and why: For six weeks I logged four things every day: how many hours I slept, how much time I spent on my phone, how many steps I took, and how I'd rate my mood from 1 to 5. Sleep and screen time came from my iPhone automatically. Mood I just wrote down at the end of the day. The idea was to see if any of these things actually correlated with each other, or if I'd just end up with a spreadsheet that said nothing.

What I found: The relationship between screen time and sleep was way more consistent than I expected. Every week where my screen time was higher, my sleep was shorter - not just once or twice, but across all six weeks without a single exception. Week 3 was the clearest example: highest average screen time, lowest average sleep. Mood followed the same pattern. I built the charts weekly instead of daily because the day-to-day noise was hiding the actual trend - that was probably the most useful thing I learned about data visualization from this project.

What I'm doing with it: I set a phone cutoff at 10 PM after looking at this data. I knew screen time was affecting my sleep in some vague way, but seeing it charted out across six weeks made it feel different - less like a suspicion and more like a fact I couldn't really argue with. That's what good data communication is supposed to do, and it was kind of cool to experience it on myself.
